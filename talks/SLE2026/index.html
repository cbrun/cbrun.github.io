Act 3

We confused expressivity with usability.

(pause)

So the next lesson is more uncomfortable.

We were not wrong to make languages more expressive. We needed that expressivity.

But adoption at scale made another problem impossible to ignore.

We kept making languages more expressive.

But users were often asking for something else.

For years, a lot of our energy went into precision, coverage, extensibility, and tooling power.

And none of that was irrational. These languages were used for serious engineering work.

But many users were asking for something more basic: help me understand what to do. Help me find the right thing. Help me trust that the tool is still responding. Help me collaborate without having to understand the whole technical environment.

What we call the User Experience as a whole.

A familiar desktop archaeology

Many useful concepts.

Sirius Desktop was based on Eclipse, with the full set of IDE metaphors around it.

When you spend years in that environment, you see the richness of these metaphors: perspectives, views, editors, properties, commands, builders, validators, problems, preferences.

Most of them are useful.

But for a domain expert, or even for many engineers, the experience can feel like an aircraft cockpit.

A very concrete example is the Eclipse workspace. There is a zone where you import or create projects. It is not necessarily the place where files are stored on disk.

It may need to be refreshed when something changes outside Eclipse.

For us, that was normal. For many users, it was just cognitive load.

A familiar desktop archaeology

We had to hid Eclipse.

In several customer projects, for instance this one with MAIF, part of the work was to hide Eclipse: the workspace, the perspectives, the menus.

We kept only the navigation that made sense from the modeling tool. The goal was not to deny the technical richness underneath. The goal was to stop asking every user to understand it before they could do useful work.

(transition)

And this is where an important distinction became visible.

None of these choices were irrational.

Most of them were useful.

But usefulness does not automatically create usability.

There is another example I find very revealing.

For years, one of the topics that came back again and again from Capella users was auto-layout.

It has an immediate impact on the user experience. People see it. They feel it. It affects whether the model is readable and whether people are willing to work with it.

And yet, over many years, almost nobody funded significant progress on that topic.

Everyone agreed that user experience mattered. But when priorities were set, resources naturally went to precise, domain-specific capabilities.

(pause)

That says something about our maturity as an industry. We know how to explore technical solutions. We are less disciplined about bringing them all the way to the user.

(pause)

We often confused expressivity with usability.

(transition)

Act 4

2019.

Fast forward to 2019.

Sirius Desktop had become a strong platform. It covered a lot of what I described at the beginning: graphical editors, tables, forms, viewpoints, queries, and integration with the Eclipse ecosystem.

But the world around it had changed.

User expectations had changed. Modeling was often perceived as heavy, not practical enough, not agile enough, and too difficult to share in everyday collaboration.

Around 2015, it was already clear that the Web was becoming a major platform for serious applications. Browsers were capable of things that would have seemed unrealistic a few years before.

For us, 2019 was not the beginning of the question. It was the moment when the question became a public decision.

And for modeling tools, the Web was an obvious answer to many collaboration problems.

But the hard part was not why. The hard part was how.

I was convinced modeling had to become more accessible. I was much less certain we could rebuild the foundations required to make it happen.

Between 2015 and 2018, we tried prototypes. Some were useful. Some were false starts.

The technology world was a jungle, and our choices would engage us for five, ten, maybe fifteen years.

How much compatibility should we keep? What belongs on the client? What belongs on the server?

And, more personally, could a small company like Obeo rebuild enough of what IBM, Borland, Eclipse, EMF, and GMF had given us?

Not everything. But enough. Enough to build serious modeling tools again.

The decision was not “move to the Web”

It was to rebuild the assumptions around modeling tools.

Sirius Desktop Successive prototypes Experience report Sirius Web platform

By 2018, the ideas were becoming clearer. By the end of 2019, we had something concrete enough to publish as open source.

But the path was not direct. Before the experience report, there had been several prototypes, experiments, and false starts.

What we wanted to preserve from Sirius was essential: the visual dimension, the ability to iterate quickly, and the flexibility brought by queries over the language.

the EMF runtime: still a great brick, especially in a more constrained environment where we could avoid the classic pitfalls.

This is what the Sirius Web experience report documents: not only the architecture, but the rationale behind the choices we made while moving from an Eclipse workbench to a Web platform.

But we did not create a separate Web team starting from zero. We trained the people who already carried the Eclipse and modeling experience, and let them bring that experience into the new world.

That takes time. But it also preserves memory.

One Eclipse assumption no longer holds in the same way: that power can justify complexity.

In the Eclipse ecosystem, a lot of complexity was acceptable because it gave access to a lot of power.

On the Web, that tolerance is much lower. The experience must be more immediate. The power is still necessary, but it has to be exposed differently.

And this is not a one-time design decision. In Sirius Web, we are still iterating on very basic elements of the diagram experience.

For example, we are around the fourth iteration on key interactions such as the contextual palette and the creation of links.

Sometimes this means unlearning habits inherited from the Eclipse frameworks. And sometimes, just as importantly, it means rediscovering why those habits existed in the first place.

That is what rebuilding foundations really means. You do not only move technology. You re-open decisions that had become invisible.

Trust

Performance is part of the language experience.

Fast enoughthe tool disappears Uncertainthe user hesitates Slowthe action is avoided

I wanted to insist on performance here, because in modeling tools performance is often discussed as an architecture topic.

Of course it is an architecture topic. But it is also an experience topic.

When a response time depends too much on the size of the model, the tool becomes unpredictable.

An action is instant on a small model. Then it takes eight seconds. Then thirty seconds. Then it feels blocked.

And the user does not necessarily know what changed. Did I do something wrong? Is the tool frozen? Is the model too large? Should I avoid this command?

At that point, the problem is no longer only latency. The problem is trust.

This is why, in Sirius Web, we care so much about patterns that work with very large models: asynchronous dialogs, filtering, pagination, queries that do not assume that everything is locally available, and UI choices that remain predictable at scale.

From editor to platform

A language needs a platform to live.

Language Experience Collaboration Capitalization

This is the other shift I would describe.

There is a difference between porting Sirius to the Web and rethinking Sirius for the Web.

Porting means rebuilding the diagram, the palette, the properties, the explorer, the actions. That was necessary. But it was not sufficient.

Rethinking means accepting that the diagram is no longer the whole story.

First, the language still matters. Its concepts, its constraints, its semantics. Without that, there is no foundation.

A language needs its definition, of course. But it also needs an experience: diagrams, trees, tables, forms, details views, and guidance adapted to the work people are doing. This is how the user discover the language and manipulate it.

It needs collaboration, because the model is no longer only a local file opened by one expert. It becomes something people discuss, review, share, and connect to other work. And collaboration have an impact into how you design the model.

And it needs capitalization: libraries, publication, reusable assets, APIs, extension points, and workflows that let an organization build on what it has already learned.

(pause)

when you think about what I said in the begining and what we were told was required for languages, then this gives a slightly different picture.

That is why I'd like to see Sirius Web less as a diagram editor in a browser, and more as a platform for modeling experiences.

Open standard

SysML v2 reopens the ecosystem.

A new standard also means new practices, new tools, and new choices.

SysML v2 changes the context in a deep way.

It is not only a new version of SysML. It is probably one of the largest opportunities for renewed adoption that modeling languages have seen in a long time.

(pause)

Several things realign at the same time. The standard evolves. Tools have to be rethought. Users have to re-evaluate their practices. Organizations that hesitated with MBSE can start again from a more modern base.

And for the SLE community, SysML v2 brings language engineering questions back to the center: explicit semantics, textual and graphical syntax, APIs, interoperability, analysis, transformation, and automation.

It also brings new extension mechanisms: a grounded core, an extensive standard library, and new ways to specialize the language without leaving the standard.

From a language design POV, this is also, AFAIK, the first language normalizing both a textual and graphical syntax.

(pause)

Being a significant actor in the MBSE - Model Based System Engineering world, we were following the directions and developments of this standard with interest. So we created SysON.

Modeling is cool again. Modeling, but revisited.

This is also why we started the SysON project with CEA. The goal is not only to have one more SysML v2 tool. The goal is to make sure that a durable, supported, extensible, open-source platform exists for SysML v2 while the ecosystem is still being formed.

(pause)

Without that kind of platform, I think many promises of SysML v2 will remain theoretical. A specification is necessary, but it is not enough. People need a way to practice the language, to experiment, to teach it, to extend it, to integrate it, and to build industrial workflows around it.

(pause)

And putting this under open source, under the EPL, is not just a licensing detail. It changes where the value has to be.

If the common ability to model in SysML v2 is available as an open platform, then every actor in the ecosystem has to position its value on more precise things: domain extensions, integration, analysis, methods, services, expertise, collaboration, certification, or deployment at scale.

For me, that is healthy. It raises the baseline. And it gives the SysML v2 ecosystem a better chance to remain open while it grows.

Not after the choices are locked in.

This timing matters a lot.

When a new standard appears, the ecosystem is not immediately stabilized. People are still learning what the language means in practice. Organizations are still deciding how they want to use it. Tool vendors are still deciding what kind of experience they want to provide.

That moment does not last forever.

After a while, tools stabilize. Training material appears. Extensions are written. Formats, habits, integrations, and procurement decisions become harder to change.

This is what SysON makes possible.

With UML and SysML v1, open source initiatives like Topcased, Papyrus, and others played a major role. But they often arrived in ecosystems that were already structured.

Commercial tools were installed. Methods, formats, extensions, training, and toolchains had already formed around them.

With SysML v2, the timing is different. The standard is new. Practices are still forming. Tools are still appearing. Organizations still have choices to make.

The goal with SysON is to make a modern, Web-based, extensible, open source SysML v2 modeler available while the ecosystem is being formed.

Not after the battle. While the choices are being made.

best of both worlds

Arcadia/Capella over SysON

With SysML v2 and SysON, I do not think the only future is to build pure DSLs completely separated from standards. There is another possibility.

We can customize the SysML v2 experience itself. We can provide an Arcadia-oriented library. We can customize the editors, the explorer, the views, the actions, the vocabulary exposed to the user.

Under the hood, it is still SysML v2. The standard is still there. The interoperability story is still there.

how does it look ? We built a SysMLv2 library for Arcadia, and an extension for Sirius Web.

Beyond pure DSLs

Customizing the experience does not mean leaving the standard.

Under the hood: SysML v2. For the user: a method-aware, Capella-like experience.

From our experience with Capella I shared earlier, one thing is very clear: methodology matters. Capella was adopted not only because there was a metamodel, or because there were diagrams. It was adopted because the tool embodied a way of working. It gave system engineers a vocabulary, a progression, and a set of views that made sense for their practice.

But for the final user, the experience can be much closer to Capella: more guided, more methodological, closer to the way system engineers already think.

Under the hood, the model is still SysML v2. But the user does not have to start from the generic standard experience.

The explorer, the diagrams, the available actions, the vocabulary, all of that can be shaped by a method.

This is the kind of ecosystem movement I would like to see. Not a choice between standard purity and useful methodology. And not a return to closed, isolated tools.

With a platform like Sirius Web, we can move value higher. Value can be in methods, domain extensions, integrations, analysis, collaboration, and user experience, while the common modeling substrate remains open and standard.

Arcadia is one methodology, you might have a slightly different one and tool it on top of SysON and SysMLv2, or tool it on top of a native DSL.
